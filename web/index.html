How to read this

This tool infers seaborne commodity activity by watching where ships stop. A vessel that berths at a single-purpose terminal is counted as a port call for that terminal's commodity. It measures activity, and pairs it with customs data for tonnage.

activity index = departures (7d) ÷ median weekly departures (12-week baseline)
z-score = (departures 7d − baseline mean) ÷ baseline std. dev.
A terminal flags when |z| ≥ 2 — unusually busy or quiet versus its own norm.

Activity is a proxy, not verified tonnage

Concentrate, cathode, and dedicated tanker or gas flows give a strong live signal. Containerised commodities such as lithium chemicals do not — those lean on customs data instead.

Customs data lags 1–3 months

National sources (Cochilco, SUNAT) lead the picture for the home market; UN Comtrade follows.

AIS coverage is uneven

Good near coasts and receivers, with gaps offshore. Transponders can be switched off, so absence of a signal is not absence of a ship.

Berth purity matters

Trust high-purity, single-source terminals. Treat general multi-commodity ports as noisy unless activity is tagged at the berth level.

Lithium prices are indicative

There is no clean free real-time reference price for lithium; the benchmark indices are paid. Read the lithium figure as directional only.

Alerts wait for a verified berth

A terminal can read as elevated (dashed ring) while its berth polygon is still an approximate placeholder. Email alerts arm only once the polygon is checked against a marine chart — until then the map flags the signal but holds the alert.
